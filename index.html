Public Transport Accessibility Across Melbourne

By Avishneel Sagar Narayan

Melbourne's public transport network is dense in some places and thin in others. This data story compares stops, modes, population and area to ask a simple question: where does access look strongest, and where might residents need more attention?

1. Introduction

Start with the shape of the network

Train and tram lines create strong corridors, while bus services spread access across far more suburbs. The first two maps show the difference between route structure and stop-level access.

The network map shows that rail and tram infrastructure is concentrated around strong radial corridors, while many outer areas depend on lighter, more dispersed route coverage.

At stop level, the picture becomes denser: buses fill many of the gaps between fixed rail corridors, making them central to everyday suburban coverage.

The mode count comparison confirms this imbalance: bus stops dominate the network by volume, even though trains and trams often shape how people imagine Melbourne's public transport system.

2. Stop locations

Stop density reveals the inner-city advantage

Counting stops by area highlights where public transport is physically close together. This favours compact inner SA2s, so it should be read as a spatial intensity measure rather than a complete accessibility score.

The highest stop density appears in compact inner and middle suburbs, where many stops are packed into a small area and walking distances between services are shorter.

Splitting the map by mode shows why density alone is not enough: bus coverage spreads widely, while train and tram stops create more selective corridors of higher-capacity access.

3. Mode distribution

Having stops is not the same as having choices

Some areas have many stops but mostly one mode. Others have fewer stops but benefit from a stronger mode mix, especially where train or tram services sit close to buses.

The stacked bars highlight that high stop totals can hide dependence on one mode, especially where bus stops make up most of the local network.

The coverage dot plot makes mode choice clearer: areas with multiple modes have more resilient access than places served almost entirely by a single transport type.

Service timing adds another layer to access, with activity clustering around the day peaks and showing that the usefulness of a stop also depends on when services run.

4. Population vs access

High population does not guarantee high stop supply

Normalising by population changes the picture. A suburb can look well supplied in raw stop counts but less generous once the number of residents is considered.

When stops are compared against population, some low-population areas stand out strongly, while larger residential areas can look less well supplied per resident.

The scatterplot reinforces the mismatch: population and stop supply increase together only loosely, so high demand does not always align neatly with high stop provision.

5. Final access score

A simple score brings the signals together

The final score combines stops per resident, stops per square kilometre, mode diversity, and rail/tram availability. It is not an official measure, but it helps compare areas consistently and makes the trade-offs visible.

The ranked bars identify the clearest extremes, separating areas where multiple access signals stack up from those where the network appears thinner.

The final map brings the story back to geography: stronger access clusters around the denser transport core, while outer areas show where distance, lower density, and fewer mode choices combine.
